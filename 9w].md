# Class Notes: Elastic Stiffness and Generalized Hooke's Law

## Introduction to Deformation and Strain

- **Deformation**: A material can undergo deformation when subjected to external forces. One common type of deformation is **elongation**, where a material is stretched by a certain length `x`.

- **Relative Deformation (Strain)**: The measure of deformation relative to the original length is called **strain**, denoted by `ε`. It is defined as:

  $$
  \varepsilon = \frac{x}{L}
  $$

  - `x` = change in length (elongation)
  - `L` = original length of the material

  This is a dimensionless quantity that quantifies how much a material is stretched or compressed relative to its original size.

---

## Generalized Hooke's Law

- **Hooke's Law**: This fundamental law in mechanics relates the **force** applied to a spring to its **displacement**. In the simplest case, it is expressed as:

  $$
  F = kx
  $$

  - `F` = force applied
  - `k` = spring constant (stiffness)
  - `x` = displacement

- **Generalized Hooke's Law**: In solid mechanics, this law is extended to relate **stress** (σ) to **strain** (ε) through the **Young's modulus** (E), a material property that describes its stiffness:

  $$
  \sigma = E \varepsilon
  $$

  - `σ` = stress (force per unit area)
  - `E` = Young's modulus (elastic modulus)
  - `ε` = strain (dimensionless)

  This relationship is the **constitutive relationship** of linear elasticity.

---

## Calculating Elastic Stiffness

- **Objective**: To determine the **elastic stiffness** (i.e., the spring constant `k`) from the **constitutive relationship**.

- **Approach**:
  - Start with the constitutive law:  
    $$
    \sigma = E \varepsilon
    $$
  - Combine with the definition of strain:  
    $$
    \varepsilon = \frac{x}{L}
    $$
  - And the definition of stress:  
    $$
    \sigma = \frac{F}{A}
    $$
    - `A` = cross-sectional area of the material

  - Substitute these into the constitutive law to express force in terms of displacement:

    $$
    \frac{F}{A} = E \cdot \frac{x}{L}
    $$

  - Solve for force:

    $$
    F = \frac{E A}{L} x

    $$
    F = k x

    $$

# Class Notes: Elastic Stiffness of a Beam

## Introduction to Elastic Stiffness

Elastic stiffness, denoted as $ k $, is a fundamental concept in structural mechanics. It represents the resistance of a material or structure to deformation when subjected to an external force.

---

## Definition of Elastic Stiffness

Elastic stiffness is defined as the ratio of the applied force to the resulting displacement:

$$
k = \frac{F}{\delta}
$$

Where:
- $ F $ is the applied force
- $ \delta $ is the resulting displacement

---

## Derivation of Elastic Stiffness for Axial Loading

When a force is applied along the **axial direction** of a beam, the elastic stiffness can be expressed in terms of material and geometric properties:

$$
k = \frac{Y \cdot A}{L}
$$

Where:
- $ Y $ is **Young's modulus** (a material property)
- $ A $ is the **cross-sectional area** of the beam
- $ L $ is the **length** of the beam

The cross-sectional area $ A $ can be expressed in terms of the beam's width $ w $ and height $ h $:

$$
A = w \cdot h
$$

Thus, the stiffness equation becomes:

$$
k = \frac{Y \cdot w \cdot h}{L}
$$

---

## Key Observations

- The elastic stiffness $ k $ is **inversely proportional** to the length $ L $ of the beam.
- It is **directly proportional** to the cross-sectional area $ A $ and the Young's modulus $ Y $.
- This relationship shows that stiffness depends on **both material properties** and **geometric dimensions**.

---

## Complexity of Non-Axial Loading

While the calculation of elastic stiffness for axial loading is **simple and straightforward**, the situation becomes **significantly more complex** when forces are applied in directions **not aligned with the beam's axis**.

For example:
- Forces acting **perpendicular** to the beam axis (bending)
- Forces that induce **torsion** or **shear**

These cases require more advanced formulations, such as:
- **Bending stiffness** (involving the moment of inertia and flexural rigidity)
- **Shear stiffness**
- **Torsional stiffness**

These topics will be covered in later sections.

---

## Summary

- Elastic stiffness $ k $ is defined as the ratio of force to displacement.

A **torque** is applied to the beam, which can be composed of multiple forces acting at different distances from the pivot point. Specifically:

# Class Notes: Stiffness and Width Variation Relationship

## Key Concepts

- **Width Variation**: The width (W) of a material or structure can vary by ±150 nanometers, which is equivalent to ±15 microns.
- **Relative Width Variation**: This is defined as $ \frac{dW}{W} $, and it is approximately ±10% in this context.
- **Stiffness Proportionality**: The stiffness (K) of a material is proportional to the **cubic** width, i.e., $ K \propto W^3 $.

---

## Mathematical Derivation

Given the relationship between stiffness (K) and width (W):

$$
K \propto W^3
$$

We can express this as:

$$
K = C \cdot W^3
$$

Where $ C $ is a constant of proportionality.

Taking the differential of both sides:

$$
dK = C \cdot 3W^2 \cdot dW
$$

Dividing both sides by $ K $:

$$
\frac{dK}{K} = 3 \cdot \frac{dW}{W}
$$

This shows that the **relative variation in stiffness** is **three times** the **relative variation in width**.

---

## Interpretation

- If the **relative width variation** is ±10%, then:
  
  $$
  \frac{dK}{K} = 3 \cdot \left( \pm 10\% \right) = \pm 30\%
  $$

- Therefore, the **relative stiffness variation** will be **±30%**.

---

## Summary

| Parameter | Value | Meaning |
|---------|-------|---------|
| Width (W) | ±150 nm (±15 microns) | Absolute variation in width |
| Relative Width Variation ($ \frac{dW}{W} $) | ±10% | Proportional width uncertainty |
| Relative Stiffness Variation ($ \frac{dK}{K} $) | ±30% | Derived from cubic dependence of stiffness on width |

---

## Conclusion

- The **stiffness of a structure is highly sensitive** to variations in its width due to the **cubic relationship**.
- A small **±10% variation in width** can lead to a **±30% variation in stiffness**, which must be accounted for in design and analysis.

# Class Notes: Stiffness of Folded Beams

## Overview

This section discusses how the stiffness of a single beam changes when multiple beams are arranged in series (folded configuration), and how this affects the overall stiffness of the system.

---

## Key Concepts

### 1. **Stiffness of a Single Beam**

- The **stiffness** of a single beam is directly proportional to its **width** and **height**, and inversely proportional to its **length**.
- Widening the beam increases its stiffness.

---

## 2. **Stiffness of Folded Beams (Series Configuration)**

- When multiple beams are arranged **in series**, the **overall stiffness decreases** compared to a single beam.
- For example, if we have **n** beams in series, the **overall stiffness** becomes **n times smaller** than that of a single beam.

---

## 3. **Achieving Equivalent Stiffness with Folded Beams**

- To achieve the **same stiffness** as a single beam, when using **n** folded beams, the **width** of each individual beam must be **increased**.
- The **required width** of each folded beam is **larger** than the width of the single beam by a factor of:

  $$
  \text{Factor} = \sqrt[3]{n}
  $$

  - This is because stiffness scales with the **cube** of the width (assuming similar geometry and material).

---

## 4. **Example: Two-Folded Beam**

- For **n = 2**, the required width of each folded beam is:

  $$
  \text{Width} = \sqrt[3]{2} \times \text{Original Width} \approx 1.26 \times \text{Original Width}
  $$

- This means each folded beam must be **~26% wider** than the original single beam to maintain the same stiffness.

---

## 5. **Example: Five-Folded Beam**

- For **n = 5**, the required width of each folded beam is:

  $$
  \text{Width} = \sqrt[3]{5} \times \text{Original Width} \approx 1.71 \times \text{Original Width}
  $$

- This means each folded beam must be **~71% wider** than the original single beam.

---

## 6. **Benefits of Folded Beams**

- **Minimizes Process Variability**: By increasing the width of each beam, the impact of **process spread** (variations in dimensions) is reduced.
- **Enables Mass Production**: This approach allows for the fabrication of **thousands of devices** with consistent performance.

---

## Summary

- A single beam has higher stiffness due to its dimensions.
- When beams are folded in series, the overall stiffness decreases.
- To maintain equivalent stiffness with folded beams, the **width** of each beam must be increased by a factor of **cube root of n**.
- This design is useful for **reducing process variability** and enabling **mass production** of microdevices.

## Overview of Overall Stiffness

- **Overall Stiffness** is calculated as the **stiffness of a single element multiplied by the number of springs** in the system.
- This concept is illustrated in simpler cases, such as **in-plane motion**, where the width of the structure can be adjusted to accommodate desired spring values.

---

## Design Considerations for Spring Elements

### 1. Avoiding Single Beam Elements

- **Single beam elements** are not recommended due to their **sensitivity to process variations** (process spread).
- Instead, **folded spring structures** are preferred for better **tolerance to process non-uniformities**.

---

## Stiffness Calculation with Multiple Springs

### 2. Stiffness of Multiple Suspended Springs

- If a system has **N springs**, the **overall stiffness** is given by:

  $$
  K_{\text{total}} = N \times K_{\text{single beam}}
  $$

- Example: If there are **4 suspending springs**, the total stiffness is **4 times** that of a single beam.

## Effect of Folding on Stiffness

### 3. Folded Spring Structures

- When a spring is **split into multiple subfolds**, the **stiffness decreases** proportionally to the number of folds.

  $$
  K_{\text{folded}} = \frac{K_{\text{single beam}}}{F}
  $$

- This design is **more robust** against **process variations** (e.g., non-uniformities in fabrication).
